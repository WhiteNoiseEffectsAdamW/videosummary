DESIGN DECISIONS & REASONING
================================================================================

EMAIL DIGEST — TWO-TIER CARD HIERARCHY
---------------------------------------
The most important video in a digest gets the full treatment: TL;DR, quote,
topic timestamps with descriptions, and a verdict line. Less important videos
get a compact row: channel, title, one-line summary, and a contextual note.

Why: The brand promise is editorial curation. A flat list of identical cards
says "we processed these." A tiered layout says "we read these and here's what
matters." The hierarchy itself is the product value.

EMAIL DIGEST — NO PRESCRIPTIVE LANGUAGE
----------------------------------------
Early draft had "One worth watching, two you can skip" as the intro and
"Skip it" verdicts on individual cards. Both were removed/rewritten.

Why: The brand positioning is "Your attention belongs to you. Not to an
algorithm." Telling users to skip a video is Headwater playing algorithm —
the exact thing the product is supposed to replace. The verdict line now gives
context ("Familiar if you've read Deep Work. Summary covers it.") without
commanding action. The user decides.

EMAIL DIGEST — LIGHT PALETTE ONLY
-----------------------------------
The email uses #fafaf7 warm off-white with #fff cards and #b8924a amber.
No dark mode variant.

Why: Emails need to survive every client (Gmail, Outlook, Apple Mail). Dark
mode emails frequently break. The warm off-white avoids sterile Gmail-white
while remaining universally safe. The amber accent shifts slightly darker
(#b8924a vs #c4a35a) because amber that works on black doesn't automatically
work on white.

EMAIL DIGEST — SUBJECT LINE LEADS WITH CHANNELS
-------------------------------------------------
Changed from "3 new videos from your channels" to
"Inside Dentistry, Cal Newport — 3 new videos"

Why: Leading with a count is what every newsletter does. Channels first gives
the user a reason to open it — they see who posted before how many. The count
becomes supporting detail, not the headline.

VERDICT FIELD IN PROMPT
------------------------
Added to src/prompts/summarize.js:
  "verdict": "<one sentence: who this video is most useful for and whether
  it's worth the full watch. Be specific about the audience. Never say 'skip'
  or tell the reader what to do — just give context.>"

Why: The email digest needs a one-line contextual note per video. The existing
schema (tldr, topics, quotes, categories, titleClaim) covers the summary but
not the "who is this for" angle. This is additive — no existing fields changed.
The instruction explicitly prohibits prescriptive language to match the brand
principle established above.

SERIF LEAK FIX
---------------
Changed .pricing-price from font-family: var(--serif) to var(--sans), and
from font-weight: 400 to 700 with -0.02em letter-spacing.

Why: EB Garamond is confined to exactly two contexts: nav brand name and
landing page h1. The pricing card prices were a third context, decorative
rather than editorial, which weakened the rule. Inter bold at 36px with tight
tracking is visually strong enough for prices without breaking the typography
system.

READ/UNREAD ROW DIFFERENTIATION
---------------------------------
Added .vrow.unread (left 2px amber border, font-weight 600) and .vrow.read
(dimmed title in --text-3, dimmed channel in --text-4). Removed inline
style="color:var(--text-3)" overrides from video row titles in favor of
class-based styling.

Why: The original mockup used the same surface treatment for every video row.
The only visual difference was an inline color override on "read" titles —
a hack, not a system. Unread rows now have a left amber accent border that
draws the eye without being loud. Read rows dim both title and channel name.
This is information hierarchy: a human thought about which rows matter more.

SUGGESTED CHANNELS — DASHED BORDER
------------------------------------
Changed .popular-chip from solid border + filled background to dashed border +
transparent background. Hover transitions to solid border + amber tint fill.

Why: Followed channels (channel-row) and suggested channels (popular-chip)
used the same surface treatment, making them visually equivalent. Dashed
border on transparent signals "not yet added" — a suggestion, not a fact.
The hover transition (dashed → solid, transparent → amber tint) previews
what "following" would look like.

EYEBROW LABEL HIERARCHY
-------------------------
Split into two tiers:
- Brand eyebrows (landing-demo-trust, digest-eyebrow): 13px, weight 600,
  letter-spacing 0.04em, uppercase, accent color. These are marketing
  moments — "THE VIDEO, NOT THE TITLE" and "EVERY MORNING."
- Product labels (account-section-title, popular-label): 12px, weight 600,
  letter-spacing 0.02em, sentence case (not uppercase), --text-3 color.
  These are form labels — "Profile," "Plan & billing," "Suggested channels."

Why: The original mockup used the same 11px/700/0.1em/uppercase treatment on
every label regardless of context. This is a classic generated-design tell —
one token applied uniformly because the system doesn't distinguish importance.
Brand eyebrows should feel different from form labels because they serve
different purposes.

LIGHT LANDING AS DEFAULT
--------------------------
Swapped the mockup switcher: light landing is now the first/default tab,
dark landing moved to last tab as "Landing (Dark)."

Why: The light variant is stronger for the landing page. The warm off-white
palette (#fafaf7 / #f2efe8) with dark CTA buttons (#1a1a1a) is more
distinctive than amber-on-black. Light mode serves the emotional argument
("upstream of the algorithm" = calm, editorial) while dark mode serves the
product utility (replacing YouTube's interface). This is the Notion/Linear/
Readwise pattern: editorial for marketing, focused for the tool.

KEY DESIGN DECISIONS (these are settled — don't relitigate them):

1. PALETTE: Amber-on-near-black for the product. Warm off-white for landing.
   - Product accent: #c4a35a (on dark surfaces), #b8924a (on light surfaces)
   - Product backgrounds: #0a0a0a / #1a1a1a near-black

2. TYPOGRAPHY: EB Garamond in exactly two places — nav brand name and landing
   page h1. Nowhere else. This is a hard rule. Pricing prices were leaking
   into a third context (decorative serif on $0/$15) and have been fixed to
   Inter bold. Don't reintroduce serif outside those two contexts.

3. LIGHT LANDING / DARK PRODUCT split is intentional. The landing page
   (off-white, dark CTAs) is the editorial/marketing face. The product
   (near-black, amber) is the focused tool. Notion/Linear/Readwise pattern.
   Don't unify them.

4. NO PRESCRIPTIVE LANGUAGE. The brand promise is "your attention belongs to
   you." Verdicts give context, never commands. "Familiar if you've read Deep
   Work. Summary covers it." — not "Skip it." This is baked into the prompt
   instruction in src/prompts/summarize.js.

5. EMAIL DIGEST — TWO-TIER HIERARCHY. Featured video: full treatment (tldr,
   quote, topic timestamps, verdict). Secondary videos: compact rows (channel,
   title, one-line summary, verdict). Subject line leads with channel names
   ("Inside Dentistry, Cal Newport — 3 new videos"), not count.

6. READ/UNREAD DIFFERENTIATION. Unread video rows: 2px left amber border,
   weight 600. Read rows: dimmed title (--text-3) and channel (--text-4).
   Class-based, not inline styles.

7. SUGGESTED CHANNELS vs FOLLOWED CHANNELS. Suggested (popular-chip): dashed
   border, transparent background — signals "not yet added." Followed
   (channel-row): solid surface. Hover on suggested previews what following
   looks like (solid border, amber tint fill).

8. EYEBROW LABEL HIERARCHY. Brand eyebrows (marketing moments): 13px, weight
   600, 0.04em tracking, uppercase, accent color. Product labels (form labels):
   12px, weight 600, 0.02em tracking, sentence case, --text-3 color.
